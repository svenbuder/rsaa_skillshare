# Finding Research Questions & Supervisors

RSAA Skillshare — February 2026

---

## What this session is about

This session is about:

- how research projects *actually* take shape
- how to think about research questions as evolving objects
- how to refine questions so they become doable and meaningful

This session is **not** about finding a perfect question.

---

## Who this session is for

The same skills apply at different stages:

- **Honours**: turning an interest into a first workable question
- **Early PhD**: refining a question so the project becomes tractable
- **Later PhD**: sharpening questions to structure papers and chapters
- **Postdoc thinking**: generating new projects by reframing questions

You are not expected to be at the same stage — only to practise the same skill.

---

## Research questions are tools

Research questions are not only for choosing a project.

They are tools for:

- thinking clearly about a problem
- deciding what to do next
- structuring papers and chapters
- evaluating whether work is actually answering something

Good researchers use questions continuously, not just at the start.

The details differ across theory, observation, and computation, but the role of questions does not.

---

## Topic, question, project

A useful distinction:

- **Topic**: the area you work in  
- **Question**: what you want to understand or test  
- **Project**: what you actually do in the next weeks or months  

You can have a topic without a question. Many projects stall because the topic is clear but the question is not.

You cannot have a project without *some* question.

---

## A common misconception

> “Good research projects start with clear, clever questions.”

**Reality**:

- most good projects start with confusion or discomfort
- clarity is an *output* of research, not an input
- your first version of a question is expected to be bad

---

## Where good questions come from

Good questions often start when:

- something does not match expectations
- two methods or datasets disagree
- a model works “except when…”
- a simplifying assumption feels too convenient
- a new dataset enables a comparison that was not possible before

If something bothers you scientifically, that is often the seed of a question.

---

## What makes a question useful

A useful research question:

- can be stated in 1–2 sentences
- implies what would count as an answer
- can produce a *first result* in weeks, not years
- is still useful even if the answer is “nothing changes”

Broad questions are usually under-specified, not ambitious.

Examples of first results a.k.a. excellent first proof-of-concept plots for a computing/observing/grant proposal:  

- a comparison plot  
- a failed assumption check  
- a sanity test on one dataset  

---

## Questions are meant to be refined

A typical refinement path:

- “I want to study X”
- “How does X behave in regime Y?”
- “Why does X fail under condition Z?”

Refining a question is not failure.  
Refusing to refine a bad question is.

---

## Projects are sequences of questions

A research project is rarely one big question.

It is usually:

- a sequence of increasingly better questions
- where each answer informs the next refinement

This is also how papers are structured:

- the introduction motivates specific questions
- the results address them
- the discussion revisits each question explicitly

---

## Why supervisors matter for projects

Supervisors affect:

- how often you get feedback
- how decisions are made
- how much uncertainty you deal with day to day

These differences shape how your project feels, not just what it is about.

---

## Common supervision patterns

Most supervisors lean toward one of these patterns:

- **Very involved**: frequent meetings, detailed feedback, clear next steps
- **Hands-off**: infrequent meetings, broad guidance, high independence
- **Big-picture focused**: strong on framing, papers, connections; fewer details
- **Detail-focused**: strong on methods, correctness, and precision

None of these are inherently good or bad.

---

## The key question is fit

Different students and projects need different things.

What matters is:
- how much structure you need *right now*
- how comfortable you are with uncertainty
- how much feedback helps you make progress

The same student may want different styles at different stages.

---

## How to figure out what works for you

Pay attention to:

- when you feel most productive vs most stuck
- whether you benefit from frequent feedback or time alone
- how you respond to open-ended vs concrete advice

Talking to current or past students often tells you more than titles or CVs.

---

## What you will practise today

You will practise:

- identifying scientific tension
- writing questions that can be wrong
- deliberately shrinking scope
- testing whether a question is useful before you invest months in it

The goal is progress, not perfection.
